Fairness and Bias in AI-driven News Curation and Public Discourse

A systematic review of how bias enters AI-driven news platforms — from training data to algorithmic design to user feedback loops — and what mitigation strategies the field has converged on. Synthesised across 17 peer-reviewed articles published between 2022 and 2024.

Why an SLR for AI bias?
AI-driven news curation is a fast-moving and contested field — the oldest paper in this review is from 2022, the bulk from 2023–2024. A single empirical study captures one slice of one platform at one moment in time; a systematic review synthesises convergent evidence across studies, identifies where the field agrees and where it doesn't, and surfaces the gaps that should shape future research. For a domain whose downstream effects include political polarisation and the erosion of shared discourse, that synthesis is the responsible methodology.

01 — Research Context

The way most people receive news has changed — quietly, structurally, and at scale

News dissemination today is driven by AI algorithms that search, curate, and distribute content across social media platforms. A growing number of people also rely on large language models (LLMs) to stay informed about current events. This represents a fundamental shift in how public discourse is shaped: not by editors making explicit choices, but by recommendation systems optimised primarily for engagement.


When algorithms learn that polarising content drives interaction, they surface more of it. When they pick up societal biases from their training data, they amplify them through mass distribution. The downstream effects — echo chambers, filter bubbles, the misclassification of underreported stories as non-factual, and the spread of opinion disguised as fact — are no longer hypothetical. They are documented and measurable.

When unchecked, algorithmic bias does not stay an engineering problem — it becomes a problem of democratic legitimacy.

This review asks how that bias enters the system, where it does the most damage, and what design and policy levers are emerging to push back.

02 — Research Questions

Three questions that structured the search

These were defined before the corpus was assembled — a methodological requirement of systematic reviews that prevents confirmation bias and ensures coverage of the full evidence space.

RQ01
How are biases generated and perpetuated in AI systems used for news curation and distribution?
RQ02
What ethical considerations should be kept in mind while designing AI algorithms to ensure fair and balanced news curation and distribution?
RQ03
How do AI-curated news feeds impact people — and how much damage can these biases cause to public discourse and democratic life?
03 — Search Strategy & Selection

A focused search across three databases and a three-year window

Because this is a recent socio-technical concern, the search targeted papers from 2022 onward — when AI-driven news distribution and LLM-based news consumption began to dominate the academic conversation. The strategy was deliberately narrow rather than encyclopaedic: the priority was depth of evidence on the specific intersection of AI platforms, news dissemination, and political discourse.

04 — Analysis Framework

Four sources of bias — where the problem actually enters the system

Across the corpus, a consistent four-part taxonomy emerges. Bias is not a single failure mode at a single point in the pipeline — it enters at the human, data, algorithmic, and interaction levels, and these layers compound. Naming each source precisely is the precondition for designing mitigations that actually work.

👤
Human bias

The people who design, train, and maintain AI systems carry conscious and unconscious beliefs that influence data selection, label choices, and architectural decisions — embedding societal prejudices directly into the system before it ever sees an end user.

  • Developer assumptions about what is "newsworthy"
  • Choices in training-data sourcing and curation
  • Subjective decisions during data cleaning
📊
Data bias

AI systems learn from vast historical corpora. When that data reflects existing inequalities, stereotypes, or under-representation of certain groups or viewpoints, the model does not correct for it — it replicates and often amplifies it.

  • Historical inequalities baked into training corpora
  • Underrepresentation of minority viewpoints
  • Cleaning choices that quietly skew what's "valid"
⚙️
Algorithmic bias

Bias also emerges from the algorithm itself. Recommendation engines optimised for engagement push content that aligns with users' pre-existing beliefs, producing filter bubbles and echo chambers as a structural consequence — not as an accident.

  • Engagement-driven ranking favours reinforcement over diversity
  • Feedback loops compound exposure to a narrowing slice of views
  • Optimisation targets that reward extremity over nuance
🔄
User-interaction bias

Systems learn from what users do. When users engage primarily with biased content — for any reason — the system reads that as preference and serves more of the same. The user's interaction is not free of context; it is shaped by what the algorithm already chose to show them.

  • Engagement signals interpreted as endorsement
  • Polarised content begets polarised recommendations
  • The "preference" the system learns is partly its own creation
05 — Key Findings

What the evidence reveals about real-world impact

Algorithmic
Engagement-optimised curation is structurally biased — not incidentally so
Recurring across 8+ reviewed studies on recommendation systems
Most major platforms prioritise engagement metrics — likes, shares, comments, dwell time — as the primary signal for ranking content. The evidence is consistent: this design choice makes algorithms structurally vulnerable to reinforcing existing user beliefs, because engagement is highest when content confirms what a user already thinks. The result is not a neutral feed with occasional bias; it is a feed whose architecture is biased toward whatever the user has already engaged with.
Design implication: Engagement is a useful metric but a poor sole optimisation target. Systems that include explicit diversity or novelty signals in their ranking function show measurable reductions in filter-bubble formation in the studies reviewed.
Sociopolitical
Polarisation effects are measurable, not abstract — including along gender lines
Reveilhac & Eisner (2022) — Twitter discourse during the Swiss Women's Strike
An empirical analysis of Twitter discourse around the Swiss Women's Strike found a statistically significant growing gender-based polarisation in political beliefs, with men and women increasingly divided in their stated worldviews on gender equality. The polarisation correlated with platform usage patterns and was not present at the same magnitude in offline survey data — pointing to the role of algorithmic amplification, not just self-selection.
Why it matters: The downstream consequences extend beyond opinion. The same study links these divergences to voting behaviour, interpersonal relationships, and broader social cohesion — making the algorithmic layer a public-interest concern, not a UX one.
LLMs
Large language models amplify hidden biases at scale when generating news content
Fang et al. (2023) — examination of news produced by LLMs
Fang and colleagues examined the bias profile of news articles produced by leading LLMs and found systematic skew in framing, source selection, and entity portrayal — biases that mirrored those in the underlying training corpora but were now being introduced into fluent, authoritative-sounding output at scale. Related work (Venkit et al. 2023) showed measurable nationality bias in how AI-generated articles characterised people from different countries, with consistent patterns across multiple models.
The new attack surface: LLMs do not invent bias — they package it. The output looks neutral, journalistic, and confident, which makes the bias harder to detect than in legacy news. This is the most novel risk surfaced in the corpus.
Discourse
Underreported narratives can be mis-flagged as non-factual — silencing them further
Synthesis across multiple studies on AI moderation and credibility scoring
A more subtle finding: when a story has limited prior coverage — common for stories from marginalised communities or regions with thinner media presence — credibility-scoring algorithms have less corroborating evidence to draw on and are more likely to flag the story as unreliable. The mechanism is not malicious, but the effect is. Underreported narratives become further underreported.
Counterpoint: The same mechanism has legitimate protective uses against fabricated content and coordinated misinformation. The challenge is calibration, not removal — distinguishing "no prior coverage because the story is fake" from "no prior coverage because the story is from a community the mainstream press undercovers."
06 — Mitigation Strategies

What the field has converged on — prioritised by strength of evidence

These strategies are drawn from the conclusions of the reviewed papers and from the practices of organisations actively working on bias mitigation. They cluster naturally into three tiers — by strength of evidence, by demographic impact, and by how mature the implementation is across major platforms.

High priority · strong evidence
Algorithmic transparency — make the decision visible to the people affected by it
Surfacing how recommendations are produced — why a particular article reached a particular user — consistently emerges as the single most foundational mitigation. It is the precondition for user trust, regulatory accountability, and meaningful research access.
High priority · strong evidence
Bias-detection frameworks at the curation phase
Techniques like word-embedding analysis are used to identify underlying biases in the news-selection phase — catching skew at the moment content enters the recommendation pipeline rather than after it has already reached users.
High priority · LLM-specific
Continuous re-evaluation of LLM outputs
Because LLMs absorb biases from user feedback and from new training data, a one-time audit is insufficient. The literature converges on ongoing, automated evaluation cycles — particularly for systems generating news-style content at scale.
Medium priority · user-facing
Source flagging for political leaning and reliability
Surfacing the political position and reliability of a news source on the article itself empowers users to read in context. The evidence on behaviour change is mixed, but the floor effect — labelling provides information regardless of whether the user acts on it — is well established.
Medium priority · long-term
Media-literacy education for end users
No amount of technical mitigation removes the user's responsibility for critical evaluation. Studies highlight that media literacy — particularly understanding how recommendation algorithms work and how biases manifest — meaningfully improves users' resistance to misinformation.
Medium priority · governance
Multi-stakeholder accountability for bias mitigation
Lancaster et al. (2024) found that AI professionals view accountability for bias mitigation as collectively distributed yet often unowned in practice. Effective mitigation requires clear ownership across product, research, legal, and policy roles — not just an ethics review at launch.
07 — HCI Design Implications

Translating the evidence into specific interaction patterns

Systematic reviews are valuable in proportion to what they let designers actually do. These are the concrete interaction patterns the corpus points toward — moves that bring transparency and user agency into the everyday news-reading surface, rather than leaving them as policy abstractions.

  • 01
    "Why am I seeing this?" — surface the recommendation rationale
    A short, plain-language explanation attached to every recommended item — surfaced on demand, not buried in settings. Tells the user which signals contributed (topics followed, recent engagement, demographic priors) and lets them mute any of them. This is the single most direct application of the transparency principle to a UI surface.
  • 02
    User-facing diversity controls — let people steer their information diet
    A simple control — a slider, a toggle, a "show me more from outside my usual sources" affordance — gives users explicit agency over the diversity-versus-personalisation trade-off. Importantly, defaults matter more than the controls themselves. The control's job is to expose the trade-off; the default's job is to be honest about what the system is doing.
  • 03
    Cross-viewpoint exposure — design the surface, not just the recommendation
    Rather than relying on the ranker alone to introduce diversity, dedicate UI real estate to cross-viewpoint content: a sidebar of "stories from outside your usual sources," a counter-perspective surfaced alongside a contested article. The design lever is separating exposure from engagement-derived ranking.
  • 04
    Fairness-audit dashboards — make the bias detection legible to non-engineers
    Internal dashboards that surface bias metrics in terms product teams, policy staff, and regulators can read — not just data scientists. Translating a word-embedding analysis result into "this topic is over-represented for this demographic by N%" is itself an HCI problem, and the literature is thin on solutions.
  • 05
    Trust calibration cues for LLM-generated content
    When news content is partly or fully generated by an LLM, the user needs an honest signal — not a single binary label, but a layered indication of provenance, source verification, and model confidence. This is the youngest of the recommendations and the one where convergent design patterns have not yet emerged.
08 — Limitations & Reflection

What this review can and cannot conclude

Acknowledging the boundaries of an SLR is not a weakness — it is methodological honesty. These limitations also point to where future primary research and review work should focus.

Study limitations

  • 01A corpus of 17 articles, while systematically selected, is small — sufficient to identify convergent themes but limited for fine-grained meta-analytic claims.
  • 02The field moves faster than peer review. Significant LLM-bias developments in 2024–2025 are under-represented in the academic literature and were captured here partly through grey-literature sources.
  • 03Geographic and linguistic coverage skews toward English-language, Western platforms. Bias dynamics on Chinese, Russian, or African platforms may differ meaningfully and are underrepresented.
  • 04No standardised bias-measurement framework exists across the reviewed studies — different papers operationalise "bias" differently, which limits direct comparability.

What I would do differently

  • →Apply a formal quality-assessment tool (CASP, MMAT) to each study, so evidence strength is rated rather than treated uniformly across sources.
  • →Expand the grey-literature inclusion to capture industry transparency reports, regulator filings, and recent legal settlements (e.g. the AP News class-action settlement on AI-related discrimination) as primary sources.
  • →Supplement the SLR with primary user studies on transparency interventions — the design recommendations are well-supported theoretically but empirically thin on behavioural impact.
  • →Conduct a longitudinal pass — revisiting the same questions 12 and 24 months later — to track how mitigation strategies hold up as platforms iterate.
09 — Future Directions

Where the evidence base points next

Three trajectories stand out across the corpus. First, LLM-specific bias mitigation — because the failure modes of generative models differ from those of ranking systems, the methods that work on one do not straightforwardly transfer to the other. Bias-detection at the curation phase needs an LLM-equivalent at the generation phase, and the literature is only beginning to develop one.


Second, empirical work on transparency interventions. Most of the design recommendations in this review — "why am I seeing this?" labels, diversity controls, fairness dashboards — are well-motivated but under-tested behaviourally. We need user studies that measure whether these interventions actually change consumption patterns and trust calibration, or whether they primarily serve a legitimating function.


Third, emerging legal and regulatory frameworks. The AP News-reported settlement on AI-related discrimination and the Amnesty International report on predictive policing both signal that bias in algorithmic systems is moving from a research concern to a regulated one. Future reviews should integrate the policy literature alongside the academic and industry sources — the design space for mitigation is shaped by all three.
