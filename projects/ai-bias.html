AI Bias in News Recommendation Systems

A systematic literature review and critical analysis of algorithmic bias in AI-driven news platforms — proposing design guidelines for transparency, fairness auditing, and user-centred bias mitigation in intelligent interactive systems.

Project overview

When the news you see is curated by an algorithm, who decides what's worth seeing?

News recommendation systems shape political discourse, public attention, and shared understanding at scale — yet they are designed and audited primarily for engagement, not for fairness or representational balance. This project investigates how algorithmic bias enters AI-driven news platforms, what its downstream effects on users are, and what HCI design patterns can mitigate it without compromising the value of personalisation.

Approach

I conducted a systematic literature review across recent research on recommender system bias, surfacing recurring failure modes — popularity bias, demographic filter bubbles, and the under-representation of minority viewpoints — and mapping them to the interaction points where users encounter their effects.

From that synthesis I derived a set of design guidelines targeting three concerns: transparency in how recommendations are produced, mechanisms for ongoing fairness auditing, and user-facing controls that let people steer their own information diet rather than simply consume what the system serves.

Transparency
Surfacing why an article was recommended — not just that it was — so users can reason about the system's choices.
Fairness auditing
Continuous monitoring of representational balance across viewpoints and demographics, with intervention thresholds.
User-centred mitigation
Putting controls in the user's hands — preference resets, diversity sliders, and visible filter state.
