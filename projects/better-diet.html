Better Diet
Designing for behaviour change

A mixed-methods research study exploring the barriers people face when trying to improve their diet — culminating in an AI-powered nutrition tracking system and smart tableware.

Summary

We ran a multi-phase research programme — survey (n=50+), semi-structured interviews, competitive analysis, and two rounds of prototype testing — to understand why people struggle to eat well, even when they want to. The core finding: the barrier isn't knowledge, it's cognitive load and motivation. People know what they should eat; they're too tired to act on it. We translated five research-backed insights directly into product decisions, designing the Better Diet App and smart tableware to reduce friction at every step of the dieting journey.

01 — Problem Space

Why do people fail at dieting, even when they genuinely want to change?

Poor diet is one of the most addressable causes of preventable illness, yet consumer behaviour persistently lags behind intention. Existing tools either overwhelm users with data or require time and discipline that real life rarely accommodates. Our design challenge was to move beyond awareness and actually reduce the friction between intention and action.

We structured our work using a hybrid Double Diamond / UCD model — broad, exploratory research in the first two phases, then convergent prototyping and validation in the second two. This ensured every design decision was traceable back to a research finding.

02 — Research Design

A mixed-methods approach — understanding both the what and the why

We deliberately combined quantitative and qualitative methods, using each to compensate for the other's blind spots. Survey data revealed patterns and scale; interviews revealed the lived experience and emotion behind those patterns. Prototype testing closed the loop between insight and design.

03 — Discovery Findings

The data: intention is high, action is blocked

The interviews deepened this picture. Participants weren't unaware of what healthy eating looks like — they found that processing food packaging labels, planning meals, and consistently logging intake all demanded cognitive resources they didn't have at the end of a long day. The tool adoption data reinforced this: no single app commanded meaningful loyalty, suggesting existing tools don't solve the real problem.

Key observation on the competitive landscape

Of survey respondents who did use a diet tool, usage was split roughly equally across smartwatches (19%), phone apps (14%), and Fitbit (14%). No tool dominated — confirming a genuine market gap rather than a preference for any single solution.

04 — Insight Synthesis

Five insights that drove every design decision

These aren't summaries of data — they're the reframed, actionable insights that shaped the product direction. Each is grounded in multiple data points across survey, interview, and secondary research.

01 Cognitive load
Fatigue is the #1 enemy of dietary intent
The biggest barrier isn't willpower — it's decision fatigue. By evening, people lack the mental energy to research meals, count macros, or plan ahead. Any solution must minimise the number of decisions required.
"I don't want to spend time reading packaging when I'm already tired." — Interview participant
02 Motivation
Progress visibility sustains behaviour change
Survey respondents who had stuck to dietary changes cited visible progress as a key driver. Streaks, social validation, and seeing nutritional gaps all function as external motivators when intrinsic motivation dips.
"I always end up procrastinating on my diet. It's a motivation thing." — Zak, 22, interview participant
03 Friction
Manual tracking is a known failure point
The most common reason users abandon calorie-tracking tools is the effort of manual logging. Food scanning via AI removes this entirely — users photograph a meal and the system does the rest.
This was the central innovation unlocked by our research.
04 Social
Social accountability amplifies personal commitment
Participants noted that knowing others were watching their progress made them more consistent. Shared recipe discovery and streak visibility create a lightweight accountability layer without requiring explicit coaching.
05 Discovery
People want to enjoy food, not just manage it
A recurring theme in both the survey and interviews was that healthy eating shouldn't feel like a restriction. Respondents were drawn to the idea of discovering recipes they'd actually enjoy, not just foods that met a calorie target.
"If I could find something healthy that I actually like, I'd be less tempted by junk food." — Interview participant
05 — Research → Design

How each insight became a feature

This traceability — from a research observation to a concrete product decision — is the core value of embedded UX research. Each feature below exists because the data demanded it, not because it sounded appealing.

  • 📷
    From: Insight 01 & 03 — fatigue + manual tracking
    AI food scanner eliminates manual calorie logging
    Users photograph food on the smart plate; the AI identifies ingredients and logs nutritional data automatically. The plate's weight sensor provides precision without any user input. Zero effort, accurate data.
  • 🍽️
    From: Insight 05 — enjoyment over restriction
    Personalised recipe engine fills nutritional gaps with food users like
    Rather than presenting a list of "healthy" foods, the ML recommendation engine surfaces recipes tailored to taste preferences and dietary goals — prioritising gap-filling nutrients while matching cooking skill level.
  • 🔥
    From: Insight 02 — progress visibility
    Streak system and dashboard provide real-time feedback on progress
    The dashboard surfaces macro/micronutrient breakdowns, hydration levels, and activity data at a glance. Streak counters and friend activity create low-stakes accountability that doesn't require active participation.
  • 👥
    From: Insight 04 — social accountability
    Social layer enables opt-in accountability without pressure
    Friends can see each other's streaks and share recipes. Importantly, social features are entirely opt-in — a direct response to HiFi testing feedback where some users did not want social elements in a health context.
  • ⚙️
    From: Insight 03 — friction in tracking
    Smart tableware removes the "before meal" decision point entirely
    The plate's weight sensor and darker measurement zone guide the user passively — no buttons, no configuration. Wireless charging makes maintenance invisible. The IoT system works in the background while the user eats normally.
06 — Prototype Testing

Two rounds of testing — what validated, what needed rethinking

LoFi testing shaped the HiFi prototype (notably, water tracking was added after multiple participants raised it unprompted). HiFi testing with 10 participants then exposed real tensions between our design assumptions and user reality.

✓ Validated
"The recipe recommendations are really useful — I always struggle to figure out what to cook that's actually healthy."
The core value proposition — contextual, personalised recipes — resonated strongly. This confirmed that our Insight 05 reading was correct and worth investing further in.
✓ Validated
"I like that I can see my friends' streaks. It makes me feel like we're doing this together."
Social layer passed the test for users who opted in. The framing as "shared progress" rather than "comparison" was key to positive reception.
⚠ Tension identified
"When I've just cooked dinner I'm too tired to wait a second to eat — taking a photo feels like one more thing."
The act of scanning food before eating introduced friction at the highest-fatigue moment of the day — precisely the context our research identified as the core problem. Future exploration: under-cupboard cameras or passive RFID scanning.
⚠ Tension identified
"The app feels cluttered — there's too much going on at once."
Designing for a wide user base meant we tried to serve too many needs simultaneously. Proposed resolution: modular onboarding where users select which features to enable, keeping the default view minimal.
⚠ Tension identified
"I don't want social functions in my health apps — it feels too personal."
Privacy in health contexts is non-negotiable for a significant portion of users. This shaped our decision to make all social features fully opt-in, with an entirely viable solo experience.
✓ Validated
"I like that there's no button on the plate — it just works."
The zero-input tableware concept was well received. Passive, invisible technology that fits existing behaviour (placing food on a plate) is the right UX direction for this context.
07 — Measurement Framework

How we would measure success post-launch

Applying Google's HEART framework to map the user experience metrics we'd track in production — connecting UX outcomes to the behaviour change goals identified in research.

H
Happiness
App store rating, post-meal NPS, satisfaction surveys after 30 days
E
Engagement
Daily active scans, recipe saves per week, streak length distribution
A
Adoption
Onboarding completion rate, first scan within 24h of signup
R
Retention
Day 7 / Day 30 return rate, streak restart rate after a missed day
T
Task success
Scan-to-log time, recipe completion rate, manual input fallback rate

The most important leading indicator would be scan-to-log time — if AI food recognition reduces the friction of logging to under 10 seconds, we've solved the core problem identified in research. All other metrics are downstream of that.

08 — Ethical Considerations

Research surfaced vulnerable use cases — and we designed for them

Health applications carry particular ethical weight. An app that gamifies food and tracks calories can cause real harm to users with eating disorders or restricted medical needs. We identified these risks proactively and built in structural mitigations.

Eating disorders & body image
Calorie goals are treated symmetrically — undereating and overeating trigger the same neutral nudge. The app avoids "deficit" language and frames progress in terms of nutritional balance rather than restriction. An 18+ age rating is recommended for launch.
AI recommendation accuracy
ML-generated meal suggestions carry a risk of allergen errors at scale. We proposed an algorithmic allergen filter as a safety layer, plus a persistent disclaimer that professional dietary advice takes precedence over AI recommendations.
Privacy & health data
Nutritional data is sensitive. Default design stores data locally; social features (which require server-side data) are fully opt-in. Users are recommended not to include medical conditions or other sensitive identifiers in their profiles.
Future: clinical expert review
We flagged the need for formal consultation with a dietitian or eating disorder specialist before any commercial launch, to validate that the app's design patterns don't inadvertently enable harmful behaviours.
09 — Reflection

What the research taught us — including about the research itself

What worked well

  • Using interviews to follow up on survey responses bridged the qual/quant gap effectively — the barrier data from the survey became emotionally textured in interviews.
  • Running LoFi testing before HiFi prototyping prevented us from building the wrong thing at high fidelity. Water tracking was added because participants raised it unprompted.
  • Grounding the tableware design in the zero-input principle (no buttons, no configuration) was validated by testing — passive technology in high-fatigue contexts is the right call.
  • The Double Diamond / UCD hybrid gave us permission to diverge broadly in Empathise/Define before converging, which prevented premature lock-in to the wrong problem.

What we'd do differently

  • Our survey skewed heavily toward 18–24-year-old males. A larger, stratified sample would have strengthened generalisability — especially for older demographics with more complex dietary needs.
  • We would use screened strangers rather than friends and family for interviews, to reduce social desirability bias in responses.
  • The smart device question in the survey should have used checkboxes rather than free text — post-processing open-ended answers introduced noise and reduced comparability.
  • We would conduct expert interviews (a dietitian, a behavioural psychologist) early in the Define phase to pressure-test our problem framing before ideating solutions.
  • A diary study over 1–2 weeks would have revealed actual eating patterns rather than self-reported estimates, giving us richer behavioural data.
