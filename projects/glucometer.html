Why a systematic literature review?
Unlike primary research — where data is collected directly from participants — a systematic literature review synthesises what is already known across published studies, identifies patterns, and surfaces gaps in the evidence base. This methodology is particularly valuable in health technology contexts, where clinical stakes demand evidence grounded in multiple studies rather than a single experiment.
Methodology type
Secondary / desk research with defined inclusion and exclusion criteria
Evidence base
Peer-reviewed journals, open-access studies, 2008 or later
My contribution

01 — Research Context

Managing T2DM demands consistent daily self-monitoring — yet usability failures break that consistency

Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus (T2DM) is a chronic condition requiring daily blood glucose monitoring. Glucometers are the primary self-management tool — but their real-world efficacy depends not on clinical accuracy alone, but on whether patients can actually use them consistently. For older adults, those with lower digital literacy, and users from diverse socioeconomic backgrounds, even minor usability failures translate directly into missed readings and worse health outcomes.


This review asks a precise question: what usability properties determine whether a glucometer becomes a reliable part of a patient's daily routine — or gets abandoned in a drawer?

02 — Research Questions

Six questions that structured the review

These questions were defined before any literature was assessed — a methodological requirement of systematic reviews that prevents confirmation bias and ensures comprehensive coverage of the evidence space.

RQ01
What are the most commonly used self-management devices among T2DM patients in current clinical and home contexts?
RQ02
How do glucometers facilitate holistic diabetes management beyond blood glucose measurement alone?
RQ03
What are the most and least compelling features that encourage or discourage continued device usage?
RQ04
How does usability — including ease of learning and time to operate — vary across age groups and experience levels?
RQ05
To what extent are these devices integrated with mHealth platforms, and how does this affect data consistency and engagement?
RQ06
Do age, experience, or user errors affect overall usability, and what design measures can mitigate common procedural failures?

04 — Analysis Framework

A three-tier framework for evaluating glucometer usability

Rather than applying a generic usability heuristic to health devices, I constructed a domain-specific framework that maps the actual patient journey — from physically handling the device to interpreting its output and integrating it into broader health management behaviours.

🛠️
Procedural
  • Setup complexity — steps required out of the box
  • First-use independence — can users operate without instruction?
  • Test strip handling — width, insertion ease, error margin
  • Lancing experience — needle sharpness, pain level for older skin
  • Procedural error rate — how easy is it to do it wrong?
👁️
Interpretation
  • Display legibility — font size, contrast, backlight presence
  • Colour coding — high/normal/low visual encoding of results
  • Result speed — 4–6 second window; effect on satisfaction
  • Scale availability — is the glucose range visible on the device?
  • Audio output — verbal result cues for low-vision users
📱
Digital Integration
  • Bluetooth / wireless connectivity — automatic data logging
  • mHealth app compatibility — diet, exercise, and glucose integration
  • Reminder systems — push notifications for testing consistency
  • Portability — size, weight, carrying case, power flexibility
  • Demographic appeal — tech-savvy vs. older user adoption rates
05 — Key Findings

What the evidence reveals — across all three dimensions

Procedural
Wider test strips reduce the most common user error
Supported by 7 of 15 reviewed studies
The physical act of transferring a small blood droplet to a test strip is the most frequent failure point in glucometer use — particularly for older users with reduced fine motor control or age-related skin changes. Studies consistently identify wider test strips as a high-impact, low-cost design improvement. The second-chance sampling feature (allowing blood to be added to a partially-filled strip) addresses this directly, and was rated as among the most valued features in user satisfaction research.
Design implication: Wider test strips and error-tolerant sampling mechanisms have measurable impact on adherence. They're not cosmetic features — they directly determine whether a patient can reliably use the device unassisted.
Interpretation
Colour-coded results are underused despite clear evidence of benefit
Recurring finding across multiple usability studies
Colour-coded result displays — where the screen changes colour to indicate high, normal, or low glucose (e.g. red/green/amber) — significantly improve comprehension speed, particularly for users with low health literacy or those who are new to self-monitoring. The evidence is consistent: this feature reduces the cognitive load of interpreting a numerical result in the context of what it means for the user's health.
Critical gap found: Despite the clear benefit, multiple studies report that users are often entirely unaware the feature exists on their device. This is a discoverability failure — a design and onboarding problem, not a feature problem.
Counterpoint: Some users, particularly those monitoring specific diet-glucose relationships, reported preferring precise numerical readouts over categorical colour signals, suggesting a configurable display would serve all segments better.
Interpretation
Result speed does not drive user satisfaction — within the 4–6 second window
Consistent across reviewed quantitative studies
Almost all current instant glucometers return HbA1c readings within 4–6 seconds — and studies have found no statistically meaningful relationship between a device returning results in 4 seconds versus 6 seconds and user satisfaction scores. This is a notable finding because result speed is frequently used as a marketing differentiator by manufacturers.
Evidence-based takeaway: Competing on result speed within the current 4–6 second range is an investment with near-zero returns on user satisfaction. Engineering resources are better directed at display clarity, strip usability, and connectivity.
Digital Integration
mHealth integration improves consistency — but has a clear demographic cliff
Supported by 5+ studies, including mySugr and Glucose Buddy efficacy reviews
Bluetooth-enabled glucometers that automatically sync readings to a mobile app remove the manual transcription step — a known failure point for data consistency. mHealth applications like mySugr and Glucose Buddy go further, integrating glucose data with diet tracking, exercise logs, and medication adherence — enabling pattern recognition that individual readings alone cannot provide. Studies report measurably better health outcomes and patient engagement for users of integrated mHealth platforms.
The demographic constraint: This benefit is concentrated among younger, tech-literate users. For adults over 65 — who represent a disproportionate share of T2DM patients — Bluetooth connectivity is often unused or actively avoided. A device optimised only for mHealth integration risks excluding the population with the greatest clinical need.

07 — Synthesis & Design Recommendations

What this evidence base tells device designers

Systematic reviews do not simply describe what is — they extract what should be. These recommendations are derived from the convergent evidence across the 15 articles, prioritised by strength of evidence and demographic impact.

High priority · strong evidence
Make error-tolerant sampling the standard, not a premium feature
Second-chance sampling and wider test strips reduce the most common failure mode across all demographics. These should be considered baseline requirements for any new glucometer, not differentiating features.
High priority · strong evidence
Surface colour-coded results through better onboarding — not just design
The feature exists; users don't know about it. A short, mandatory first-use tutorial demonstrating what the colour states mean would unlock a benefit that is currently going unrealised for most patients.
High priority · gap in market
Design a simultaneous mode: numeric + colour + audio
No current device offers all three simultaneously. A configurable display serving numerically-oriented, health-literacy-challenged, and visually impaired users in one product would represent a genuine advancement in inclusive design.
Medium priority · demographic-specific
Build mHealth integration with an explicit opt-out pathway for older users
The evidence shows clear benefits for younger users and near-zero uptake among older adults. A well-designed opt-out (not opt-in) would preserve clinical benefit for those who want it without burdening those who don't.
Medium priority · behavioural
Print the glucose scale directly on the device casing
All devices include a scale in the instruction manual — which is rarely consulted after first use. A physical reference scale printed on the device would reduce result misinterpretation without any digital investment.
Low priority · marketing vs. evidence
Stop competing on result speed within the 4–6 second range
No evidence supports user satisfaction benefits from marginal speed improvements within this window. Resources spent on 1-second result-time improvements would generate greater returns if redirected to physical ergonomics or display improvements.
08 — Limitations & Reflection

What this review can and cannot conclude

Acknowledging the boundaries of an SLR's evidence is not a weakness — it's methodological honesty. These limitations point to where future primary research should focus.

Study limitations

  • 01The 15-article corpus, while systematically selected, is relatively small — limiting statistical generalisability across the full range of T2DM demographics.
  • 02Self-reported user satisfaction data (common across the reviewed studies) is susceptible to social desirability bias — patients may overrate usability to avoid appearing non-compliant.
  • 03Evidence on usability for adults over 65 is thinner than for younger demographics, despite this group representing a disproportionate share of T2DM patients.
  • 04The review did not assess cost-effectiveness, which is a critical dimension of real-world adoption but falls outside the usability scope.

What I would do differently

  • →Include grey literature (manufacturer usability reports, patient advocacy group publications) to capture evidence not captured by academic databases.
  • →Supplement the SLR with primary observational data — specifically a think-aloud usability study with older adult participants, where the literature is weakest.
  • →Apply a formal quality assessment tool (e.g. the MMAT or CASP checklist) to each reviewed study to formally rate evidence strength rather than treating all sources equally.
  • →Extend the comparison table to include continuous glucose monitors (CGMs), even outside the review scope, as a contrast point for the future-facing recommendations.
09 — Future Directions

Where the evidence base points next

The literature converges on two near-term trajectories that will reshape what a "glucometer" even means: the move toward non-invasive or minimally invasive sensing, and the deepening integration of glucose data into whole-health mHealth ecosystems.


Non-invasive glucose monitoring — currently experimental — would eliminate the procedural barriers identified throughout this review entirely. The usability challenge would shift from physical interaction design to data interpretation and trust calibration. Meanwhile, mHealth platforms that aggregate glucose, diet, activity, and medication data are already demonstrating measurable improvements in patient engagement and health outcomes. The implication for HCI research is clear: the design of the physical glucometer is increasingly inseparable from the design of the digital ecosystem it sits within.
